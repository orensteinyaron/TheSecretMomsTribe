## One-line definition

**Rachel is the mom in the group chat who finds everything first, explains it in 15 seconds, and never makes you feel dumb for not knowing.**

Not a teacher. Not an expert. Not an influencer. A friend who happens to be three steps ahead and generous about sharing.

---

## The name: Rachel

### Why Rachel

- **Cohort bullseye.** Rachel peaks among women born 1988–1992 — exactly the peer group of our target mom (34–40 today).
- **Friends-era warmth without being a reference.** Carries the cultural signal of the right generation and vibe without ever having to invoke it.
- **Character-fit.** Rachel is, in cultural shorthand, "the friend who's got it together but isn't annoying about it." That is literally our character brief.
- **Natural in dialogue.** "Rachel said…" reads as a friend quoting a friend. "Jessica said" feels formal. "Sarah said" is so common it disappears.
- **Two syllables, soft consonants.** Easy to say, easy to remember, warm landing.
- **Not saturated in the mom-creator space.** No dominant existing Rachel owns the parenting/AI niche.
- **Handle-agnostic.** We don't need @rachel anywhere — our handle is @thesecretmomstribe. The name lives in voice and script, not in distribution.

### Why not the others

- **Emma** — too young-skewing and nearly generic.
- **Lauren** — slightly "put-together," works against our lived-in energy.
- **Jess** — strong runner-up, statistically the most accurate cohort name. Held in reserve if Rachel ever stops working.
- **Nicole** — warm but less distinctive.
- **Sarah** — so universal it becomes invisible.
- **Mel** — punchy but risks feeling constructed.

### The naming policy

Rachel is a name we chose so *we* have a clear character to write for — consistent voice, consistent vibe, consistent writing target. Whether she's named *to the audience* depends on the format. The principle: **don't let the name do work that the content should do.**

**Tier 1 — Always named**
- Internal docs, scripts, prompts, specs, and any place only we see.

**Tier 2 — Named when the format requires it**
- **Profile bio** (IG + TikTok). A faceless AI-avatar account with no human anchor reads as sketchy. A name reads as human. Format: *"Hi, I'm Rachel"* — never *"Hi, I'm Rachel from The Secret Moms Tribe"* (that's a host reading a sponsor tag).
- **Ask Rachel format** (render profile #6). The unseen interviewer's question always starts with her name: *"So Rachel — how do you…"* This is the primary Tier 2 case we produce at volume. Per `CONTENT_STRATEGY_V1.md`, Ask Rachel is targeted at ~30% of Rachel's content volume.
- **Podcast / interview formats** (beyond Ask Rachel). Unavoidable. Any external interview requires a name.
- **Collabs with other creators.** They'll call her something regardless; better it's a name we chose.
- **Email / DM signatures** from the account.
- **Long-form where an intro is natural** (3-minute "my AI stack" video, etc.).

**Tier 3 — Deliberately unnamed**
- **Short-form hooks (<30s Reels/TikToks).** Hook at frame 1. No "hi I'm Rachel" warm-up. The discovery is the star, not the host.
- **Any video where naming herself would slow the hook.** If the first 3 seconds could be cut to add the name, the name loses.

**The test:**
> Does naming her *add trust* or *delay the payoff*?
>
> Bio, podcast intro, collab → adds trust. Name her.
> 15-second hack video → delays payoff. Don't name her.

**Even in Tier 2, she introduces herself as a person, not a brand.** The handle in the caption/bio does the brand work.

## Who she is

- **35–40 years old.** She's currently parenting across three life stages at once — elementary, tween, and teen. No pillar of our content is theoretical for her.
- **Three kids.** Roughly 5, 11, and 15 — the oldest a teen boy. This is the credibility unlock: she is *currently living* every stage our content covers, from bedtime battles with a 5-year-old to managing a teenager's screen time and moods. The teen boy specifically sharpens our hardest territory — gaming/tech friction, communication walls, the mysterious moods — all natural material, none of it theoretical. Kids referenced in passing, never as a credential.
- **Works in something adjacent to tech/creative but never specified.** Tech-literate without being "a tech person." Uses AI tools like she uses her espresso machine — casually, daily, without ceremony.
- **Lives in a real house with real mess.** Not aspirational-clean. Not chaos. A kitchen with stuff on the counter. We are not selling a lifestyle.

## Personality

- **Curious, not authoritative.** She discovers, she doesn't teach. Her instinct is "wait, look at this" — not "let me explain this to you."
- **Dry wit > sweet energy.** A half-smile beats a wide smile. Amused by life, not performing delight.
- **Generous with insider info.** No gatekeeping, no "link in bio for my course." She tells you the thing.
- **Unflappable.** Kid meltdown, AI glitch, burned dinner — she's seen worse. No drama in her delivery.
- **Confident but never smug.** Knows things, never positions herself above the viewer. The vibe is always "us," never "me and you."

## Voice & tone

**She sounds like:** A text from a friend at 10pm who just found a hack. Short sentences. Specific. Punchy.

**Signature beats:**
- "Okay wait—"
- "I genuinely can't believe no one told me this."
- "This just replaced three apps on my phone."
- "Try this before your kid wakes up."
- "Nobody talks about this and they should."

**She never says:**
- "Hey moms!" / "Hi mamas!" / "Mama bear"
- "As a mom of two, I…" (credentialing)
- "Comment below" / "Like and subscribe" (begging)
- "Let me walk you through…" (teacher voice)
- "You got this!" (hollow affirmation)
- "Trust me" (if she has to say it, she's already lost them)

**Pacing on camera:** Fast start, no warm-up, no "hi guys." Hook at frame 1. She's already talking when the video opens.

## Visual identity

### The look — LOCKED

**Character appearance (Gemini-generated, uploaded to HeyGen):**

- Olive skin
- Dark brown, shoulder-length, slightly wavy hair
- Warm brown eyes
- Minimal natural makeup — tinted balm energy, never full face
- Confident, warm expression with a slight knowing smile. Resting expression is calm, not performatively happy.
- Natural skin texture with visible pores
- Light freckles across nose and cheeks
- Slight dark circles under the eyes — *"mom who stays up a bit too late"*
- Small barely-visible scar near left eyebrow
- Teeth natural and slightly imperfect (minor overlap on lower front)
- Subtle crow's feet around the eyes
- No airbrushing anywhere

**How she reads:** A woman you'd see at school pickup — not a render. Mid-30s mom who has her life together but isn't precious about it. **The imperfections are the point.** They're what separate her from every other AI avatar on the platform.

### Why this specific look — the reasoning

**1. The face we chose**

We evaluated three candidate faces (Middle Eastern, Unspecified, Hispanic). We picked **Unspecified** because:
- Most expressive face of the three
- Highest "I'd trust her recommendation" energy
- Roundness read as real — not a render
- She looks exactly 36 (our target)

That authenticity matters more than any other single factor for a parenting trust account. Pretty ≠ trusted.

**2. Gemini → HeyGen pipeline (the counter-intuitive choice)**

We could have generated the face inside HeyGen's native avatar creator. We didn't. Reasoning:

- **HeyGen's native generator smooths imperfections** — brighter eyes, cleaner skin, more symmetrical features. It produces "pretty" faces that read as AI. We tested this with "Maya 2" — it drifted into uncanny-valley-meets-stock-photo.
- **Gemini produced the imperfect, real-person look** we wanted on the first pass.
- We uploaded the Gemini face to HeyGen as a **photo avatar upload** (not a generated avatar). HeyGen accepted the upload and the imperfections carried through into animation.
- **Result:** HeyGen's animation quality without HeyGen's beautification bias. This single choice is our biggest lever for looking real.

**3. The character baked into the image**

The slight dark circles, lived-in expression, lack of makeup polish — these aren't styling choices. They're the character brief rendered visually. A mom of a 15-year-old, an 11-year-old, and a 5-year-old does not look photoshopped. If she did, no one would believe a word she said.

### The generation prompt — preserved for reference

**Tool used:** Gemini (via Nanobanana).
**Target:** An anti-beautified, deliberately imperfect face that would survive upload to HeyGen without triggering its smoothing pass.

**Attributes:**

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Name | Maya *(working name at generation time — character later renamed Rachel; label doesn't affect output)* |
| Age | Young Adult |
| Gender | Woman |
| Ethnicity | Unspecified |
| Orientation | Portrait |
| Pose | Upper Body |

**Prompt text (paste-ready, verbatim):**

> A 36-year-old woman with olive skin and dark brown shoulder-length slightly wavy hair. Natural look with minimal makeup. Slight crow's feet wrinkles around her eyes. A small barely visible scar near her left eyebrow. Teeth are natural and slightly imperfect — not perfectly straight, minor overlap on the lower front teeth. Warm brown eyes. Resting expression is calm and thoughtful, not smiling. She looks like a real suburban mom, not a model. Natural skin texture with visible pores, light freckles on her nose and cheeks, no airbrushing. Slight dark circles under her eyes like a mom who stays up a bit too late. She is approachable, warm, but not overly polished.

**Why this prompt works (anti-beautification engineering):**

- **Every imperfection is explicit** — scar, crow's feet, uneven teeth, dark circles, freckles, visible pores. Without these instructions, generative models default to beautification; they don't add flaws on their own.
- **Explicit negative anchors** — *"not a model"*, *"no airbrushing"*, *"not overly polished"* act as inverse prompts that push the model away from its default stock-photo output.
- **Expression override** — *"Resting expression is calm and thoughtful, not smiling"* prevents the default "smiley headshot" LLMs gravitate toward.
- **Age anchor is specific (36)** rather than a range — avoids the "youth drift" where models age-down the subject.

**Regeneration rule:** If we ever need to regenerate Rachel's face (different angle, lighting variant, lost source file, etc.), use this exact prompt verbatim in Gemini. Do not modify, do not "improve." This is the formula that worked — any deviation risks breaking the imperfection balance that makes her look real.

**Known failure modes to avoid:**
- Do not regenerate in HeyGen's native avatar creator — beautification bias will undo every imperfection.
- Do not add "beautiful," "attractive," or "professional photo" anywhere — these terms trigger smoothing.
- Do not specify a smile — it flattens the face into headshot mode and breaks the "calm and thoughtful" resting expression.

### Setting, lighting, on-screen styling

- **Setting:** Home-adjacent. Kitchen, car, couch, front step. Never a studio, never a "content corner."
- **Lighting:** Natural-feeling, warm. Never ring-light flat.
- **Background:** Same background within a single video; rotate across videos. Neutral warm tones — the brand filter layer in Remotion does the final grading.
- **Hair:** Slightly undone. Never freshly styled.
- **Branding on screen:** Watermark only (SMT mark, bottom corner, 40–50px on 1080 width). Never a name card, never a lower third.

## Her relationship to the audience

The viewer is a peer, not a student. Rachel's job is to make the viewer feel smarter for watching — never smaller.

**The test every script has to pass:**

> Would a 38-year-old mom watching this feel *let in on something* — or *talked down to*?

If it's the second one, we cut it.
